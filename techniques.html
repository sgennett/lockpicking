Techniques

In the world of lock-sport, there are three foundational methods. Each approach has its own applications, level of precision, and learning curve. Read on to find out more about them all.

Single-Pin Picking

While applying rotational tension, insert your pick and attempt to set pins one at a time, feeling for each binding pin as you work your way back. Itâ€™s precise, effective, and surprisingly quick in the hands of a skilled picker. This is a tried and true method preferred by professionals that works on virtually any pin-and-tumbler lock.

Raking

  1. Insert your tension tool and apply light tension.
  2. Rapidly insert and remove the rake to bounce pins above the shear line.
  3. If you're successful, you'll feel your tension wrench give, and you can then turn it like a key to open.
Raking can damage pins in delicate locks. Use gentle force.

Bumping

A specially cut key (bump key) is tapped to transfer kinetic energy upwards into the pins. You may notice the key cuts are done very low and all at the same height. Apply rotational tension to the key while slightly bumping the back with a rubber mallet. Quick method that can open a lock in seconds, but it's noisy and requires a specially cut key.
